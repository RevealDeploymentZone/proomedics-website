Erectile Dysfunction and Diabetes: The Connection Explained

By Promedic Medical Team  |  July 16, 2026  |  9 min read

Erectile dysfunction and diabetes are closely linked. If you have type 1 or type 2 diabetes and are experiencing difficulty getting or keeping an erection, you are far from alone — and effective treatment is available. This guide explains why diabetes causes ED, how serious it is, and what can be done about it.

3x

Men with diabetes are up to 3 times more likely to develop erectile dysfunction than men without diabetes, and ED often appears 10–15 years earlier.

Why Does Diabetes Cause Erectile Dysfunction?

An erection depends on two things working correctly: healthy blood vessels to deliver blood to the penis, and intact nerves to trigger and maintain the response. Diabetes damages both over time, through two main pathways:

1. Vascular Damage (Atherosclerosis)

Chronically elevated blood sugar damages the lining of blood vessels (endothelium), causing inflammation and the build-up of fatty plaques — a process called atherosclerosis. The small blood vessels in the penis are particularly vulnerable. When blood flow is restricted, achieving a firm erection becomes difficult or impossible. According to the American Diabetes Association, poor blood glucose control is the single strongest predictor of ED in diabetic men.

2. Neuropathy (Nerve Damage)

Diabetic neuropathy — nerve damage caused by prolonged high blood sugar — affects autonomic nerves throughout the body, including those that control erections. These nerves signal the blood vessels in the penis to relax and fill with blood. When they are damaged, this signalling fails even if the blood vessels themselves are healthy.

How Common is ED in Diabetic Men?

Studies consistently show that 35–75% of men with diabetes will experience erectile dysfunction at some point in their lives. The NHS reports that diabetic men are also more likely to experience ED at a younger age — often appearing in their 40s rather than 60s. Men with poorly controlled diabetes, or who smoke, have high blood pressure, or are obese, face even higher risk.

Do ED Medications Work for Diabetic Men?

Yes — PDE5 inhibitors such as Viagra (sildenafil), Cialis (tadalafil), and Levitra (vardenafil) are all effective for many men with diabetic ED. However, response rates are somewhat lower than in non-diabetic men — around 50–60% compared to 70–80% — because the underlying nerve and vascular damage reduces the body's ability to respond to the medication.

Among the available options, some evidence suggests Levitra may be slightly more effective in diabetic men due to its high PDE5 selectivity. Cialis is also popular because its 36-hour window allows for more spontaneous sexual activity without the pressure of timing. Our article on Levitra vs Viagra provides a detailed comparison.

Lifestyle Changes That Help

Managing blood sugar is the most important long-term strategy. Men who bring their HbA1c into the normal range often see partial or significant improvement in erectile function — independent of any medication. Other lifestyle factors that make a meaningful difference:

  • Regular exercise — improves both blood sugar control and vascular health
  • Healthy weight — obesity significantly worsens both diabetes and ED
  • Quitting smoking — smoking accelerates vascular damage in diabetic men
  • Limiting alcohol — see our guide on alcohol and Viagra
  • Managing stress and anxiety — psychological factors compound the physical ones

When to See a Doctor

If you have diabetes and have noticed changes in your sexual function, speak to your doctor or a specialist. ED in diabetic men can be an early warning sign of cardiovascular disease — the same vascular damage affecting the penis often affects the heart. Early assessment can be genuinely important for your broader health.

Summary

Diabetes is one of the most common causes of erectile dysfunction. Vascular damage and nerve damage are the two key mechanisms. While ED medications are effective for many diabetic men, response rates are somewhat lower — which is why choosing the right medication, dose, and timing matters more. Improving blood sugar control, exercise, and lifestyle changes can make a significant difference alongside medication.
