What Causes Erectile Dysfunction at 30, 40, and 50?

By Promedic Medical Team  |  July 18, 2026  |  9 min read

Erectile dysfunction is not a single condition — it is a symptom with many different causes that change significantly depending on your age. Understanding what is actually driving your ED is the first step to finding the right treatment.

Key Takeaway: At 30, ED is most often psychological. At 40, it is a mix of physical and psychological. By 50, physical causes — particularly vascular disease and declining testosterone — are usually dominant.

Erectile Dysfunction at 30

In men under 35, physical causes are relatively uncommon. The most frequent drivers are:

  • Performance anxiety — fear of failure creates a self-reinforcing cycle. See our full guide on stress, anxiety, and ED
  • Relationship issues — unresolved conflict or early relationship nerves
  • Heavy alcohol or recreational drug use — both suppress sexual function
  • Depression — strongly associated with ED; many antidepressants also cause ED as a side effect

The full guide to ED in young men covers psychological and physical factors in greater depth.

Erectile Dysfunction at 40

By your 40s, physical factors begin to play a more prominent role:

  • Early cardiovascular disease — high cholesterol, blood pressure, and early atherosclerosis reduce blood flow. The American Heart Association identifies ED as an early cardiovascular marker
  • Type 2 diabetes — often undiagnosed at this age; ED can be the first sign. See our article on ED and diabetes
  • Declining testosterone — peaks in early 20s and declines gradually thereafter
  • Weight gain and sedentary lifestyle — obesity worsens both vascular health and testosterone levels

Erectile Dysfunction at 50

In men over 50, physical causes are usually dominant. Prevalence rises from ~12% at age 40 to over 40% by 60-70, per the NIDDK. Key contributors:

  • Established cardiovascular disease — arterial stiffness and plaque significantly reduce penile blood flow
  • Hypogonadism (low testosterone) — ~1 in 5 men over 50 have clinically low testosterone
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) — affects sexual function directly and through medications used to treat it
  • Prostate cancer treatment — surgery, radiotherapy, and hormone therapy can all affect erectile function
